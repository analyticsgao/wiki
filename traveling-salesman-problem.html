The Traveling Salesman Problem

The million-dollar question that sounds simple... but isn't

The Simple Question

Imagine you're a salesperson who needs to visit several cities. You want to:

  • Visit every city exactly once
  • Return to your starting city
  • Travel the shortest possible distance
What's the shortest route that visits all cities and returns home?

Sounds easy, right? For a few cities, it is. But as you add more cities, this becomes one of the hardest problems in computer science.

Why Is This So Hard?

The problem explodes exponentially. Look at how the number of possible routes grows:

3 Cities

2 possible routes

Easy! Check them all by hand.

5 Cities

12 possible routes

Still manageable for a computer.

10 Cities

181,440 routes

Computer can handle this in seconds.

15 Cities

43 billion routes

Takes hours or days to check all.

20 Cities

60 quintillion routes

Would take thousands of years!

50 Cities

More routes than atoms in the universe

Completely impossible.

The formula is (n-1)!/2, where n is the number of cities. This grows faster than almost any other function in mathematics!

The Approaches

üêå Brute Force: Check Every Route

  1. List every possible route
  2. Calculate the distance for each one
  3. Pick the shortest

Problem: Only works for tiny problems (under 15 cities). Beyond that, you'd need more time than the age of the universe.

üèÉ Greedy Algorithm: Pick the Closest Next

  1. Start at any city
  2. Always go to the nearest unvisited city
  3. Return home when done

Problem: Fast, but often finds routes that are 20-30% longer than optimal. Sometimes picks poorly early on.

üîß 2-Opt: Swap and Improve

  1. Start with any route (like greedy)
  2. Try swapping pairs of connections
  3. Keep swaps that make the route shorter
  4. Repeat until no improvement found

Problem: Much better than greedy, but still not guaranteed to find the absolute best route.

üí∞ The Million Dollar Prize

If you can prove whether TSP can be solved efficiently (in "polynomial time"), you win $1,000,000 from the Clay Mathematics Institute. This is called the "P vs NP" problem‚Äîone of the most important unsolved questions in computer science.

üé® Real-World Uses

TSP isn't just theoretical. It's used in: designing computer chips (routing connections), DNA sequencing, planning delivery routes (UPS, Amazon), drilling circuit boards, telescope observation scheduling, and even planning world tours for bands!

üèÜ World Records

In 2006, researchers found the optimal tour through 85,900 cities in a microchip design problem. It took them over 136 CPU-years of computation! The current record is solving a 115,475-city problem in 2023.

üß† Why We Care

TSP represents a huge class of "NP-hard" problems. If we find an efficient solution to TSP, we could solve thousands of other important problems in logistics, biology, physics, and artificial intelligence. That's why it matters so much!

The Takeaway

The Traveling Salesman Problem teaches us that:

  • Some problems are fundamentally hard‚Äînot because we're not smart enough, but because the universe of possibilities explodes too fast
  • "Good enough" often beats "perfect"‚Äîreal companies use fast approximation algorithms rather than waiting for perfection
  • Small changes make huge differences‚Äîgoing from 10 to 20 cities increases difficulty by a factor of 600 million trillion
  • The most practical solution isn't always the most elegant one
